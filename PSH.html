Permanent Supportive Housing (PSH)

Permanent Supportive Housing (PSH) is a long‑term housing model designed for individuals who experience chronic homelessness and live with disabilities that significantly impact their ability to maintain stable housing. PSH combines permanent housing with individualized, voluntary supportive services to promote long‑term stability and independence.

What “Permanent” Means

Long‑term housing

  • No time limit—participants sign leases and may remain housed as long as they follow standard lease rules.

Community‑based housing

  • Participants live in regular community units such as apartments, scattered‑site units, or single‑family homes.
  • Units must meet HUD guidelines and Fair Market Rent (FMR) standards.

Who Qualifies for PSH

Two core eligibility requirements must typically be met:

1. Documented Disability

  • The disability must substantially limit the individual’s ability to live independently.
  • Accepted documentation may include:
    • Verification from a licensed medical or clinical professional
    • Proof of SSI/SSDI benefits
    • Other approved disability verification documents

Common qualifying disabilities include:

  • Serious mental illness
  • Physical disabilities
  • Chronic health conditions
  • Substance use disorders that impair daily functioning

2. Chronic Homelessness

HUD-aligned PSH programs typically require:

  • 12 months of continuous homelessness, or
  • Four or more episodes of homelessness in the past three years totaling at least 12 months

To qualify as chronically homeless, a person must meet both the homelessness and disability criteria.

Supportive Services

PSH includes voluntary, flexible services such as:

  • Case management
  • Mental and behavioral health support
  • Healthcare coordination
  • Employment and income assistance
  • Connections to community resources

Services support stability and independence—they do not determine continued housing eligibility.

Why PSH Matters

PSH is one of the most effective long‑term strategies to end chronic homelessness because it:

  • Provides stable, permanent housing to individuals with significant barriers
  • Reduces the use of emergency shelters, crisis services, and law enforcement
  • Improves physical and behavioral health outcomes
  • Supports long‑term community stability
